Laudon, Jane P. Laudon, and Carol G. Traver. Pearson Publishing, 18th edition. Course Objectives By the end of the semester, students will be able to: • Understand and fluently use terminology, concepts, and theories related to information systems. • Analyze and effectively communicate features, capabilities, limitations, and development trends of information technologies and systems. • Understand and critically evaluate the social, political, psychological, or economic impact of digital information (e.g., social networks, privacy, copyright, crowdsourcing). • Develop scripts to restructure and analyze data. • Use software to efficiently organize and query data in personal and enterprise relational databases. • Use visualization software to facilitate decision-making and knowledge management.

Homework Assignments Students will complete FIVE individual assignments outside of class: • Coding in a Python Jupyter Notebook. • Coding in Python to interact with a database, retrieve data, summarize data, and generate visualizations. • Creating a customized AI bot from scratch. • Writing a series of SQL statements to retrieve information from an MS SQL Server database. • Using Tableau to visually convey information contained in a dataset. Details regarding each assignment will be provided during the semester. Late submissions will lead to grade reduction (see Late Submission Consequences below).    
DataCamp Assignments Students will need to complete SIX mini-courses/practices offered via Datacamp, which is online interactive learning and coding platform. An invitation link can be found in Modules to join the virtual learning environment on DataCamp. Academic Integrity All students are covered by a policy that prohibits dishonesty in academic work. Under the Honor Code, a faculty member will (or a student may) report an alleged violation to the Academic Honor Council, which investigates, adjudicates, and assigns punishment within established guidelines if a violation is verified. Students are required to pledge all written work submitted for a grade: “On my honor, I have neither given nor received any unauthorized assistance on this work,” followed by their signature. The pledge may be abbreviated as “pledged” with a signature. Electronic Recordings of Course Instruction To accommodate inevitable student absences, the instructor will record missed classes upon request. If you anticipate missing class, notify the instructor in advance. Recordings will be posted on Canvas after class. Class recordings are not a substitute for attending class — reasonable excuses are still required for absences. All classroom instruction, including student participation, is subject to recording and dissemination on Canvas. Recordings are available only to enrolled students for online learning and review. Students are expressly prohibited from capturing or copying classroom recordings by any means; violations are subject to disciplinary action. Instructors who wish to use a recording outside of class must obtain written consent of any students who are personally identifiable in the recording. Excused Absences Per the Faculty Handbook (6[B]VII), students who are absent from class to observe a religious holiday, who are absent while participating in a University-sponsored dramatic production, or who are off campus officially representing the University (e.g., intercollegiate athletics, debate tournaments, tours) will be excused from classes. Absence Due to Illness Students who are sick will also be excused from classes. False claims about illness may be regarded as a violation of the Academic Honor Code, which prohibits falsification of academic records. Students who experience a family emergency that interferes with academic performance are encouraged to contact Dr. Kyle Gillette, Associate Vice President for Academic Affairs: Student Success. Use of Generative AI AI can be a great tool to assist you, but it must be used responsibly. Using AI to evaluate and improve your work is acceptable; having AI do the work for you is not.   
Permitted Uses • Brainstorming and refining ideas. • Finding information on your topic. • Drafting an outline to organize your thoughts. • Checking grammar and style. • Generating an example to give you an idea of where to start or what the end result should look like. Prohibited Uses • Completing group work assigned to you, unless your group mutually agrees the tool may be used. • Writing a draft of a writing assignment. • Writing entire sentences, paragraphs, or papers to complete class assignments. All use of AI tools must be properly documented and cited per University academic honesty policies. Any assignment found to have used AI tools in unauthorized ways may receive a 0 or be required to be resubmitted.
